Academics

iGEM

COVID mRNA vaccines, which helped bring the world back to some levels of normalcy, fascinate me. Such innovations highlight the potential of biological sciences and have encouraged me to pursue my passion in biomedical research through programs like the International Genetically Engineered Machine (iGEM) competition.

Alongside other students interested in biochemistry, I started preparing for the global competition through learning about gene cloning, understanding PCRs, and doing Western blots. During lectures on synthetic biology, I simultaneously felt uncertain and excited to encounter brand-new terminologies, although I feared falling short in my iGEM journey. However, the difficulty of mastering each topic could not extinguish my growing enthusiasm for all kinds of bioengineering. I willingly spent many lunch breaks in our teachersâ€™ offices asking questions and reviewing lectures so that I could improve my understanding.

After being elected student captain of our team, I felt even more eager to develop our ambitious bioengineering project on designing yeast engineered with the SNF1 gene to test the possible stress-mitigating properties of obesity-reducing supplements. As the captain, I needed to have a concrete understanding of techniques to explain and provide solutions to problems that came up during our project. Consequently, I dedicated myself to increasing my scientific research skills and growing as a biomedical researcher. With each step toward our final project presentation, acknowledgements from instructors and fellow team members made me more confident in my role and approach as team captain.

Being a part of the first team to represent our school at iGEM completely changed my approach to learning. From initial confusion on topics to proactively reaching out to our instructors and eventually becoming the team leader, I cherish the profound impact of the iGEM process. Such advanced study at this level has solidified my passions at the intersections of biology and chemistry and encourages me to seek educational opportunities that take my learning further than ever.

Internship in Neuroimmunology Lab of NCCU

I became interested in Biochemistry after interning at a lab in the Institute of Neuroscience at National Chengchi University Taiwan for the summer. I was particularly intrigued by their research on dementia as I did experimental demonstrations with grad students and listened to lectures on their work and the gene APoE. I could not help but ask question after question about their methods and procedures. Specifically, I knew Biochemistry was for me when I was able to perform immunohistochemistry and western blotting. Learning these methods showed me applications of biochemical understanding in addressing diseases. The internship provided a transition from classroom and textbook to a career environment and lab work. It clarified to me that my scientific curiosity is applicable and that over time, I can convert knowledge I find fascinating into beneficial results.
